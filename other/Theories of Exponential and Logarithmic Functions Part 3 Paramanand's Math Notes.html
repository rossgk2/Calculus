Theories of Exponential and Logarithmic Functions: Part 3

2 comments
In this concluding post on the theories of exponential and logarithmic functions we will present the most intuitive and obvious approach to define the expression abab directly without going to the number ee and the function logxlog⁡x. This approach stems from the fact that an irrational number can be approximated by rational numbers and we can find as good approximations as we want. The idea is that if bb is irrational and a>0a>0 then we have many rational approximations b′,b′′,…b′,b″,… to bb and the numbers ab′,ab′′,…ab′,ab″,… would be the approximations to the number abab being defined. Inherent in such a procedure is the belief that we can find as good approximations to abab as we want by choosing sufficiently good rational approximations to bb. Thus we can see that the numbers
21,21.4,21.41,21.414,21.4142,…21,21.4,21.41,21.414,21.4142,…
are approximations to the number 22√22.

Intuitive Definition of abab

In order to make the definition described in previous paragraph more precise we start with the assumption that a>0a>0 and bb is any real number. If {bn}{bn} is a sequence of rational numbers such that limn→∞bn=blimn→∞bn=b then we define
ab=limn→∞abn(1)(1)ab=limn→∞abn
While this seems to be the most obvious approach to the theory of exponential and logarithmic functions it has two stumbling blocks:
  • We need to show that the limit in definition (1)(1) exists
  • If there are two sequences {bn},{cn}{bn},{cn} both tending to bb then we must show that limn→∞abn=limn→∞acnlimn→∞abn=limn→∞acn
Both these statements are obvious if a=1a=1. In the further development of the theory we can assume that a>1a>1 and the case 0<a<10<a<1 can be treated by noting that ab=1/(1/a)bab=1/(1/a)b where bb is rational and (1/a)>1(1/a)>1.

We first show that there is a sequence bnbn tending to bb such that limn→∞abnlimn→∞abn exists. Clearly we can choose any monotonically increasing sequence bnbn which tends to bb. By any theory of real numbers (Dedekind cuts or Cantor's constructions of reals via Cauchy sequences of rationals) it is possible to find such a sequence bnbn of rationals. In practice we may choose bnbn to consist of decimal approximations to bb adding each extra digit for each term of the sequence. Note that such a sequence bnbn is bounded above (because it is increasing and reaches a limit bb). This means that there is a rational number BB such that bn≤Bbn≤B for all nn. Now we can see that the sequence xn=abnxn=abn is increasing (because bnbn is increasing and a>1a>1) and at the same time it is bounded above by aBaB. It follows that xnxn tends to a positive limit.

The inequalities may become weak if we try to construct sequences of positive rationals tending to r,sr,s and then letting n→∞n→∞. But we don't really need the strict versions of those inequalities.
