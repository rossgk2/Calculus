Developing a good understanding of calculus comes down to developing an understanding of two key notions:

1. Infinitesimals
2. Deriving antiderivative formulas from derivative formulas

## A dream that almost came true

There are a couple possible ways to achieve (1) and understand infinitesimals. The first and most prevalent way is to treat infinitesimals as a helpful heuristic, but not as something that is really formally valid. When students learn this approach, they may be emphatically told that infinitesimals have no meaning, and that limits are "the only valid way". Teachers can be dogmatic and utterly lack creativity, sometimes.



## Making the best of it

So, in standard calculus, 

(1) turns into

1. understanding how to justify intuitive heuristic arguments made with infinitesimals by using limits. 

ex. lim_{h -> 0} (x^2 + 2xh + h^2 - x^2)/h instead of (dx^2 + 2x dx + dx^2)/dx

standard texts cover this well.

2. carefully setting up the notation of the chain rule, so that it looks intuitive and is hard to mess up. 

--

standard texts don't do this.


3. doing the same for inverse chain rule.

--

**standard texts don't do this. they don't explain why du/dx dx = du**
